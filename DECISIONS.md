# Decisions

## Part 2: Build Something Valuable

For Part 2, I chose to focus on **trust in the product** rather than convenience features. My decision was driven by two signals from the data. First, the qualitative feedback repeatedly pointed to hallucinations, weak citations, and uncertainty about whether answers could be relied on. Second, the usage data showed a strong relationship between answer quality signals and user satisfaction: uncited responses performed much worse than cited ones, and a meaningful share of usage happened in weak-context situations such as prompting without uploaded documents. That suggested the highest-value improvement was not simply adding more workflow features, but making the assistant more grounded, more transparent, and more willing to say when it does not know. For that reason, I implemented a trust pipeline with an explicit answerability check, stricter citation requirements, visible confidence metadata, and clickable citations that connect the response back to the source document.

I chose this over other reasonable options, especially persistent documents across conversations and richer structured comparison workflows, because those would have improved convenience before fixing the core reliability problem. In a legal due-diligence tool, a faster workflow is only valuable if the answer is trustworthy. Features like document memory also introduce additional product and technical complexity around context management, ownership, and the risk of the assistant using the wrong document set. By contrast, trust guardrails improve the quality of every answer immediately and create a stronger foundation for future features. With more time, my next steps would be to add retrieval-based grounding instead of sending full document text, improve document navigation with highlighting and search, and then revisit cross-conversation document reuse once the trust model and active-document UX are even clearer.
